### Historical Context

**Alice's Adventures in Wonderland** was published in 1865 by Charles Lutwidge Dodgson under the pseudonym **Lewis Carroll**. Dodgson was a mathematician and logician at Christ Church, Oxford. The story originated on July 4, 1862, during a boat trip on the River Thames with the three Liddell sisters — Lorina, Alice, and Edith. Alice Liddell, then aged ten, asked Dodgson to tell a story, and he began the tale that would become the book.

### The Rabbit-Hole

Alice's fall down the rabbit-hole is one of literature's most famous transitions from the ordinary to the extraordinary. The slow, dreamlike descent — past cupboards, bookshelves, maps, and pictures — establishes the topsy-turvy logic of Wonderland where normal physical laws don't apply. Carroll's description of the jar of **orange marmalade** ("I wonder if I shall fall right through the earth!") introduces the childlike curiosity and conversational style that define Alice's character.

### "Curiouser and curiouser!"

This deliberate grammatical error is Alice's invention — she recognizes the correct form would be "more curious" but creates her own word because she feels the situation demands it. This moment captures Alice's blend of intelligence and childish independence, a theme throughout the book.

### Dinah

Alice's cat, mentioned repeatedly throughout the first chapter, serves as her link to the real world. Alice's habit of talking to herself and addressing Dinah establishes the novel's distinctive narrative voice, which blends third-person description with Alice's internal thoughts.

### Literary Device: Dream Logic

The chapter establishes that this is a dream world where the usual rules don't apply. Doors are too small, bottles talk back, and the very concept of size is unstable. Carroll creates a world governed not by physical laws but by the associative logic of dreams, where one thing leads to another without rational connection.
